Why Text Messages Aren't Enough

Texting feels fast and informal, which is exactly why most small landlords default to it. But it has three real problems:

  1. No central log. If you have three tenants texting three different numbers — your personal phone, maybe a Google Voice number — requests are scattered across conversations. There's no single view of what's open, what's in progress, and what's resolved.
  2. No documentation trail. A text thread is not the same as a timestamped maintenance record. If a tenant ever claims a habitability issue went ignored, a text thread buried in iMessage is a weak defense compared to a timestamped ticket system showing when the request came in and when it was addressed.
  3. No structure for tenants. When tenants text, they might forget to mention the unit number, not include photos, or give a vague description that requires three follow-up messages just to understand the problem.
Legal note: In most states, landlords are legally required to respond to habitability-related maintenance within a specific timeframe (often 24–72 hours for urgent issues). If you can't demonstrate you received a request and responded, you're exposed. A documented system protects you.

What a Good System Looks Like for Small Landlords

You don't need much. A good maintenance system for a small portfolio does four things:

  • Structured intake — tenants submit requests in a consistent format (description, category, photos) rather than however a text message happens to arrive
  • Automatic logging — every request is timestamped and saved, without you having to do anything manually
  • Status tracking — you and your tenant both know whether something is "received," "in progress," or "resolved"
  • History — when you eventually sell or hand a property to a manager, you have a complete maintenance history for each unit

How to Handle Requests Professionally (The Process)

1. Acknowledge first, fix second

The single most common tenant complaint about small landlords isn't slow repairs — it's silence. Tenants want to know you received their request and you're on it. A quick acknowledgment ("Got it — I'll have someone take a look by Thursday") dramatically reduces follow-up texts and frustration, even if the actual repair takes time.

2. Categorize by urgency

Not everything is an emergency. A rough guide:

  • Urgent (respond within 24h): No heat in winter, flooding, sewage backup, electrical hazard, broken locks
  • Standard (respond within 3–5 days): Appliance issues, leaking faucet, broken window latch, pest concern
  • Low priority (can schedule): Cosmetic issues, minor squeaks, requests that aren't affecting habitability

3. Document the repair, not just the request

When a repair is complete, close the ticket with a note on what was done, who did it, and when. This builds the maintenance history that protects you legally and helps future contractors understand a unit's quirks.

4. Be consistent across tenants

One of the hidden benefits of a system versus informal texting: every tenant goes through the same process. This removes the perception (and reality) that some tenants get faster responses than others — a source of friction that can escalate quickly in multi-unit buildings.

Getting Your Tenants Set Up

The best maintenance system only works if tenants actually use it. Here's what works:

  • Send the link at move-in alongside your lease paperwork. Frame it simply: "For any maintenance issues, please use this link — it helps me track everything and respond faster."
  • Put it in your lease addendum as the official maintenance request channel. This sets expectations and creates a paper trail if a tenant later claims they reported something via text.
  • Respond faster through the system than through text — even once. Tenants will adopt whatever channel gets the quickest response.
Pro tip: When a tenant texts you a maintenance issue (and they will, at first), respond: "Got it — can you also submit it through the link I sent at move-in? It helps me track repairs properly." Most tenants will comply, especially if you respond quickly to what they submitted.

The Real Cost of Doing Nothing

The informal approach — texts, emails, memory — feels fine until it isn't. The cost shows up in three places:

  • Legal exposure: No documented response to a maintenance request is the most common basis for habitability claims and rent withholding disputes.
  • Tenant turnover: Tenants who feel ignored leave. Replacing a tenant costs a month or more of lost rent plus turnover expenses. A system that makes tenants feel heard is a retention tool.
  • Your own mental overhead: Tracking requests in your head and across three different message threads is exhausting. A system that handles this frees you up.
